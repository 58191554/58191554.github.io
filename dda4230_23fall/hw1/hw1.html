In the chart above, you can see the impact of different values of 'k'. When 'k' is small (depicted in purple and green), the algorithm learns very little from the environment during exploration. As a result, regrets increase significantly after the algorithm commits to a choice. However, as 'k' increases, the regrets after commitments tend to unchanged. This is because the algorithm becomes adept at selecting the best arm. The value of 'k' directly influences the variance. When 'k' is slightly larger, the policy's randomness diminishes (in this context, randomness refers to the convergence of policy π). The source of variance primarily lies in the divergence of policy during exploration, which occurs when there is insufficient exploration. This also explains why, when 'k' is large, the policy consistently reaches a fixed point, eliminating randomness.

This chart effectively illustrates the essence of regrets. Regret serves as a performance metric for the algorithm. In the case of ETC, with a large 'k,' regret is directly large; with a small 'k,' regret is also small, but the relative variance is substantial and tends to increase. This phenomenon follows the well-known bias-variance trade-off in machine learning.
